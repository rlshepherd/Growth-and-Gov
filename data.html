Data

Following up on the previous post about differentiating GDP growth, below I explore two possible measures of political inclusivesness: the Polity IV and World Governance Indicators datasets. This post is designed to be reproducible, and was created in R using R Studio with the markdown and knitr packages, but I had to manually edit the output to fit this blog's formatting. The code used to create this analysis can be found on my Github.

Polity IV

The first dataset comes from the Center for Systemic Peace’s Polity IV Project: Political Regime Characteristics and Transitions 1800-2010 (Marshall, 2011). This dataset contains an annual measure of a regime’s institutionalized democracy (DEMOC) and autocracy (AUTOC), as well as a third variable (POLITY2) that combines the two measures by taking their difference as well as extrapolates missing values to facilitate time-series analysis.

The Polity IV data has the advantage of describing the institutional qualities of regime along both the democratic and autocratic dimensions at the same time. The institutionalized democracy measure runs on a scale of 0 to 10. The score is base don researchers’ systematic evaluation of four subcomponents: the (1) competitiveness of executive recruitment, (2) openness of executive recruitment, (3) constraint on chief executive, and (4) competitiveness of political participation. Likewise, the institutionalized autocracy measure also ranges between 0 and 10, and is based on four subcomponents. This design is intended to deliver a picture of the overall level of public participation in government by examining the combined democracy-autocracy score (POLITY2).

The theoretical basis for the assigning democracy and autocracy scores is described in %%, and the handbook accompanying the dataset gives a detailed description of the evaluation process (link).

World Governance Indicators

A second notable source for measures of institutional quality worth mentioning here is the World Bank’s World Governance Indicators (Kauffman, Kraay, Mastruzzi 2010). The WGI consists of six country-level measures covering 1996 to 2010: voice and accountability, political stability and absence of violence/terrorism, government effectiveness, regulatory quality, rule of law, and control of corruption. The measures are constructed by categorizing aggregating various indicators including NGO reports (e.g., Freedom House), surveys (e.g., Gallup World Poll), government reports (US State Department Trafficking in people report) and business services (e.g., the Economist Intelligence Unit). A full list of sources is available on the WGI website (http://info.worldbank.org/governance/wgi/pdf/table1.pdf). Each score is also assigned a margin of error based on the number of sources (more sources translates into less error).

Of the six, voice and accountability the most useful for testing A&R’s political inclusiveness hypothesis. The authors describe the Voice and Accountability (VA) score as:

“capturing perceptions of the extent to which a country's citizens are able to participate in selecting their government, as well as freedom of expression, freedom of association, and a free media.”

While many researchers conducting cross-country regression choose to utilize the WGI dataset, it has several analytical difficulties in addition to the technical difficulties of incorporating margins of error. First, the change in scores over time may not be meaningful or comparable because sources appear or disappear for particular years and do not cover all regions (or all countries within a region). Second, several underlying source evaluations overlap or are themselves composites of other evaluations, which may lead to double counting or weighting bias. Third, the lack of a normative underpinning (by not answering the question “what is good governance?”) and reliance upon subjective sources endogenizes the factors behind effective governance. We are setting ourselves up by ascribing good governance to countries that exhibit strong economical growth, without asking what are the qualities of good governance? In this sense, measures of governance run the risk of becoming subjective measures of economic performance.

Exploring the Data with R

The Polity IV Annual Time-Series 1800-2010 dataset is available from the Center for Systemic Peace's Integrate Network For Societal Conflict Research. The entire WGI dataset is available through the WIG Project homepage, though the it requires a lot of effort to get it into time-series format. Instead, I suggest downloading the WGI's via the World Bank's World Databank API.

Here I downloaded Voice and Accountability for 2010 only, and used Excel to add a column “Year” with the value 2010 for each entry. Alternatively, you could do this in R. Either way, you'll need the “Year” column when we bind the Polity IV and WGI data together.

We can merge the two datasets, but we'll quickly discover the merge yields some bad results:

gov.data <- merge(p4.2010, wgi.va.2010, by = c("Country.Code", "Year"))

dim(gov.data)

## [1] 73  9

We only get 73 observations (matches) on the merge, down from 190 countries in the Polity IV and 213 in the WGI 2010 datasets. The Country.Code field for Polity IV uses the Correlates of War Interstate System, while the WGI data uses the ISO 3166-1 alpha-3. We can convert COW to ISO-3 using the countrycode library:

library(countrycode)

p4.2010$Country.Code <- countrycode(p4.2010$Country.Code, "cowc", "iso3c")

gov.data <- merge(p4.2010, wgi.va.2010, by = c("Country.Code", "Year"))

Bivariate Relationship

We have 154 properly labeled observations after correcting the country codes. Now we can explore the relationship between our two possible measures for inclusiveness in institutions. The car library provides a simple method for graphing enchanced scatterplots: 

library(car)

polity2.va.cor <- cor(gov.data$POLITY2, gov.data$WGI.VA.2010, use = "pairwise.complete.obs")

scatterplot(POLITY2 ~ WGI.VA.2010, data = gov.data, main = "Enhanced Scatter Plot")

The first thing I noticed the clustering at the top of plot, showing the number observations with a POLITY2 score of 10 (the maximum) but a VA score of less than the possible maximum of 2.5. Alternatively, this can be observed via the boxplot along the y-axis. One possible explination Polity IV project hasn't truely resolved the “clustering at 10” issue they mentioned in the manual. That would be bad for our study, andit would mean the VA data gives a bit more “granularity” in its assesments of democracies. However, it is important to note that the WGI data is mathetically designed to average to 0(that is, the series is normalized across a range of -2.5 to 2.5). So again, this is a reflect of the distribution of perception about countries, not an attempt to quantify the qualities of institutions directly. We should still keep the distribution of the POLITY2 variable in mind when interpretting our results.

Given the correlation coefficient is 0.8425, these two variables are telling us similar information. We definitely wouldn't include both in one model. The decision comes down to available data, and Polity IV wins by a long shot.
